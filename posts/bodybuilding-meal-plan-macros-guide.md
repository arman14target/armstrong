Training builds the stimulus. **Nutrition determines whether that stimulus becomes muscle** or just soreness and frustration.

This guide covers the macro fundamentals every bodybuilder needs — without meal-plan templates that ignore your body weight, activity level, and goals.

## Calories Come First

Before splitting protein, carbs, and fats, set your **calorie target**:

- **Muscle gain (lean bulk):** maintenance + 200–350 kcal/day
- **Fat loss (cut):** maintenance − 300–500 kcal/day
- **Recomposition:** near maintenance with high protein and consistent training

Estimate maintenance with any reputable TDEE calculator, then adjust based on weekly scale weight. Aim for 0.25–0.5% body weight change per week.

## Protein: The Non-Negotiable Macro

Protein supports muscle repair and satiety during cuts. Most lifters should target:

**0.7–1.0 g per pound of body weight** (1.6–2.2 g per kg)

Examples:

- 160 lb lifter → 112–160 g protein/day
- 80 kg lifter → 128–176 g protein/day

Spread protein across 3–5 meals. Each meal with 25–45 g protein maximizes muscle protein synthesis across the day.

## Carbohydrates: Fuel for Training

Carbs power hard sets and aid recovery. On a bulk, carbs often fill remaining calories after protein and fat targets.

On a cut, keep carbs **around training** — pre- and post-workout — to preserve gym performance.

Starting point for active lifters: **1.5–2.5 g per pound** on bulk, **0.75–1.5 g per pound** on cut (adjust per tolerance and energy).

## Fats: Hormones and Satiety

Dietary fat supports hormone production and helps you feel full. Target **0.25–0.4 g per pound** of body weight unless you prefer higher-carb, lower-fat eating and feel good doing it.

Do not drop fat below ~0.2 g/lb for extended periods without a specific reason.

## Sample Macro Targets (180 lb Male, Lean Bulk)

| Macro | Grams | Calories (approx.) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Protein | 160 g | 640 |
| Fat | 60 g | 540 |
| Carbs | 380 g | 1,520 |
| **Total** | — | **~2,700 kcal** |

Adjust portions weekly based on scale trend and gym performance.

## Meal Timing: What Actually Matters

Meal timing matters less than total daily macros for most people. Two rules help:

1. **Protein at every meal** — steady amino acid availability
2. **Carbs before/after training** — when session quality drops on low carb

Intermittent fasting works for some lifters. It fails for others who cannot hit protein targets in fewer meals. Pick the schedule you can sustain.

## Tracking Without Obsession

Macro tracking teaches portion awareness. You do not need to weigh chicken forever.

**Phase 1 (4–8 weeks):** weigh and log everything — build accuracy  
**Phase 2:** eyeball portions using prior data  
**Phase 3:** maintain habits; re-track when goals or weight shift

Apps that combine **macro tracking with workout logs** keep diet and training aligned. When your coach or program adjusts calories, you see the full picture in one place.

## Foods That Make Macro Hitting Easier

- **Lean protein:** chicken breast, Greek yogurt, egg whites, whey, tuna
- **Carb sources:** rice, oats, potatoes, fruit, pasta
- **Fats:** olive oil, nuts, avocado, whole eggs
- **Volume foods:** vegetables for micronutrients and fullness on a cut

## Red Flags in Any "Bodybuilding Meal Plan"

Avoid plans that:

- Eliminate entire food groups without medical reason
- Promise rapid fat loss (>1% body weight/week)
- Use identical calories for every body size
- Ignore training day vs. rest day needs

Your meal plan should flex with your training block — bulk, cut, or maintenance.

## Key Takeaway

Set calories for your goal, prioritize protein, fill carbs and fats based on preference and performance, then track consistently for a few weeks. Nutrition is a skill. The lifters who measure it improve it.
